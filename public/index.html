02 / RULES

Four laws.
Every cell, every generation.

The universe of Life is a two-state cellular automaton on an infinite square lattice. Each cell references its eight orthogonal and diagonal neighbors. Time advances in discrete ticks. At each tick, every cell is evaluated simultaneously against four rules — and the next frame replaces the last wholesale.

From these four primitives, an entire taxonomy of behavior emerges: still lifes that never change, oscillators that pulse, spaceships that drift, and guns that emit gliders forever. Conway proved in 1982 that Life is Turing complete — sufficient to compute anything computable.

01

UNDERPOPULATION

A live cell with fewer than two live neighbors dies. Isolation is fatal.

02

SURVIVAL

A live cell with two or three live neighbors survives. The neighborhood sustains it.

03

OVERPOPULATION

A live cell with more than three live neighbors dies. Crowding is lethal.

04

REPRODUCTION

A dead cell with exactly three live neighbors becomes alive. The neighborhood seeds it.

03 / TAXONOMY

A taxonomy of behaviors
observed across half a century.

01 STILL LIFES Patterns that never change. The block, the beehive, the loaf — stable equilibria that persist indefinitely. P1
02 OSCILLATORS Return to their initial state after a fixed period. The blinker, the toad, the pulsar — rhythmic and predictable. P2–P15
03 SPACESHIPS Translate across the grid while cycling shape. The glider, the LWSS, the Gemini — motion from stillness. C/4 · C/2 · C/12
04 GUNS Emit a stream of spaceships forever. The Gosper glider gun was the first known — and proved infinite growth. P30 · GLIDER
05 METHUSELAHS Take many generations to stabilize. The R-pentomino runs for 1103 generations before settling into 116 cells. G>1000
06 REPLICATORS Produce copies of themselves. The smallest known fits in a 12×11 bounding box and replicates every 8 generations. P8 · L>4
04 / PROVENANCE

John Horton Conway,
1970.

Late one evening in the autumn of 1970, the Cambridge mathematician John Horton Conway finished a pencil-and-paper experiment on a Go board. He had been searching for the simplest ruleset capable of producing unpredictable, lifelike behavior. The result — published in Martin Gardner's October 1970 Scientific American column — became the most famous cellular automaton in history.

Conway died in April 2020 of COVID-19. His game outlives him on every desktop, every microcontroller, every browser tab — still surprising, still Turing-complete, still exactly four rules.
